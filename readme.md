# PART 1: The Scientific Foundation

Understanding why this pipeline handles data differently than traditional sequence-based bioinformatics requires looking at how proteins evolve over long evolutionary timelines.

## 1. Sequence vs. Structural Evolution: The "Twilight Zone"

In structural biology, primary amino acid sequences mutate, drift, and undergo selection pressures far more rapidly than tertiary (3D) structural layouts.

```
[ Primary Sequence Space ] ───(Mutates Rapidly)───► Low Sequence Identity (<20%)
                                                            │
                                                   (Nature Preserves Fold)
                                                            ▼
[ Tertiary Structural Space ] ────────────────────► High Structural Match (lDDT >= 0.84)

```

Over billions of years of evolution, a housekeeping enzyme superfamily (like your deaminases) can completely scramble its primary amino acid sequence to adapt to different cellular environments, extreme temperatures, or varying substrate profiles. However, because the underlying chemical reaction still requires a precise geometric arrangement of catalytic residues, **nature preserves the spatial 3D architecture of the protein core.**

Traditional sequence alignment tools (like BLAST) fail in this range, known as the **"Twilight Zone"** (typically below 20–25% sequence identity), because they lose the statistical signal amidst the evolutionary noise. By utilizing 3D structures rather than 1D text strings, this pipeline uses physical coordinate geometries to track deep evolutionary connections that sequence-based methods cannot see.

## 2. Local Distance Difference Test (lDDT) as a Metric

Instead of relying on standard RMSD (Root-Mean-Square Deviation) to calculate structural similarity, this pipeline relies on **lDDT (local Distance Difference Test)**.

### Why RMSD Fails for Superfamily Networks

RMSD requires performing a global rigid-body structural alignment of two proteins in 3D space, then measuring the average Cartesian distance between all alpha-carbon positions. If a protein superfamily features highly dynamic, flexible domain hinges, or if an uncharacterized variant has a long, unstructured terminal loop, a global alignment will fail. The structural cores might match perfectly, but the flexible loop or shifted domain will throw off the spatial calculation, creating an artificially poor RMSD score.

### How lDDT Solves It

The lDDT metric evaluates **local atom-pair distance environments** within a single model, completely bypassing the need for a global structural alignment.

1. For every amino acid residue in a reference structure, the software maps all neighboring atoms within a specific distance threshold radius (typically $12\text{ Å}$). This creates a localized "spatial fingerprint" for that position.
2. It then looks at the target model and verifies whether those exact inter-atom distances are preserved within a strict tolerance margin.
3. Because it tracks localized neighbor distances rather than global spatial orientations, if two proteins share an identical active core but possess entirely different flexible outer loops, the lDDT algorithm will award a near-perfect score to the core residues while ignoring the outer structural variations.

An optimized threshold of **$\text{lDDT} \ge 0.84$** ensures that your network only draws an edge between two proteins if their essential backbone geometries and catalytic pockets match at a high resolution.

## 3. Network Topology and Sampling Bias

When building a Structural Similarity Network (SSN), each protein acts as a **Node**, and an lDDT score meeting or exceeding your threshold generates an undirected **Edge**.

A major challenge in network biology is **sampling bias**. Public sequence databases are heavily skewed toward organisms that are easy to culture in a laboratory environment or are medically relevant (e.g., *Escherichia*, *Bacillus*, *Pseudomonas*).

If left uncorrected, a single heavily sequenced housekeeping fold family will pull in hundreds of closely related variants. While these variants possess distinct primary sequences, they share an identical, highly rigid structural fold. In an uncorrected network, this creates an enormous **combinatorial explosion of edges**:

$$\text{Edges Within a Cluster} \approx O(N^2)$$

If you have 400 structures belonging to one dominant genus, they will generate up to $160,000$ high-scoring mutual edges. This massive cluster acts like a topological sink on a force-directed network canvas.

The physical layout engine uses a spring-embedder simulation (like stress-majorization), where edges act as attractive physical springs pulling nodes closer, and unlinked nodes exert an omnidirectional repulsive force. If 99% of your edges are trapped inside a single overrepresented core, that component will contract into an unreadable "gravitational well," flattening subtle structural variations and forcing rare, deeply branching structural families out to the legibility fringes of your plot layout.

## 4. Graph Modularity ($M$) Optimization

To solve the thresholding problem without introducing human bias, your pipeline uses an automated optimization sweep to discover the natural splitting point of your dataset. It does this by maximizing a network statistic called **Modularity ($M$)**:

$$M = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \left[ \frac{e_{ii}}{m} - \left(\frac{d_i}{2m}\right)^2 \right]$$

### Component Definitions:

* $m$: The total number of edges across the entire network.
* $e_{ii}$: The number of internal edges trapped strictly within community cluster $i$.
* $d_i$: The sum of all degrees (total connection count) for all nodes assigned to community cluster $i$.

Modularity measures the density of edges inside your clusters compared to a completely randomized control network with the exact same node degree distribution.

* **If your lDDT threshold is set too low (e.g., 0.40):** The network becomes an undifferentiated "hairball." Every node connects to almost every other node. There are no clear boundaries, edges are evenly distributed everywhere, and the modularity score approaches $0$.
* **If your lDDT threshold is set too high (e.g., 0.95):** The network fractures completely into an array of isolated nodes and tiny singletons. Natural subfamilies are split apart, and the modularity drops back toward $0$.
* **The Sweet Spot:** The pipeline sweeps through the entire threshold spectrum to locate the exact coordinate peak where your structural clusters display the maximum possible internal density alongside the cleanest possible external segregation. For your dataset, maximizing this metric isolates the exact biological boundaries separating your 49 structural subfamilies.

## 1. Stage 1: High-Speed Structural Indexing & Core Extraction

### File: `scripts/foldseek_ssn.py`

This script runs inside a dedicated Python environment (`envs/foldseek_env.yml`) to isolate your system from binary library conflicts. It acts as an abstraction wrapper around Foldseek's high-speed C++ engine.

1. **Database Instantiation (`createdb`)**: The script reads your input folder of unaligned PDB structure coordinates (`extracted_domains/`) and compresses their spatial features into a continuous structural alphabet sequence database using a local system call.
2. **All-vs-All Search Execution (`search`)**: It runs an optimized all-vs-all structural query using the command parameters:
* `-s 9.5`: Sets a highly sensitive structural search threshold to capture distant structural alignments deep within the evolutionary twilight zone.
* `--alignment-type 1`: Forces the engine to calculate explicit 3D structural residue alignments rather than relying on fast sequence-prefilter approximations.


3. **Data Serialization (`convertalis`)**: The system exports the computed alignment geometries into a tabular sheet format using explicit, structured columns: `query, target, lddt, prob, evalue, fident, alntmscore`.
4. **Matrix Serialization**: The Python script loops through the unique protein IDs and instantiates a clean, square similarity matrix populated by their mutual 3D structural match scores (`structural_similarity_matrix.tsv`), which can be directly imported into downstream data science packages.

## 2. Stage 2: Modularity Optimization and Publication Graphics R Engine

### File: `scripts/optimize_and_render_ssn.R`

This script runs within your data-visualization environment (`envs/r_graphics_env.yml`). It performs the core statistical optimization and handles plot rendering.

1. **The Optimization Loop**: The code reads your tabular alignments sheet and sweeps across the entire structural threshold spectrum from `0.40` to `0.95` in iterative steps of `0.02`. At each step, it builds a temporary graph network using `igraph` and calculates its global modularity score using the unsupervised Louvain community detection algorithm (`cluster_louvain`). It captures the exact peak coordinate that yields the highest possible modularity.
2. **Clade Assignment and Metadata Integration**: Once the optimal threshold is locked in, the script filters your edges to include only your high-confidence links, builds your final production network model, and calculates structural properties for every node:
* `group_louvain()` partitions nodes into their optimized structural clades.
* `centrality_degree()` counts how many connections each node maintains within its local cluster, serving as a direct metric for protein "hubness."


3. **Stress-Majorization Spatial Layouting**: Instead of relying on random force-directed algorithms that shuffle your network layout every time you hit run, the script uses a deterministic stress-majorization engine (`layout_with_stress`). This algorithm models your edges as physical springs and uses calculus to minimize the overall structural stress of the graph layout. This step ensures that your 49 subfamilies are arranged consistently and reproducibly on your plot canvas.
4. **Publication Graphics Engine (`ggraph`)**: The rendering pipeline constructs a multi-layered publication graphic:
* `geom_edge_diagonal0(aes(alpha = lddt))` draws your structural edges, using transparency (`alpha`) to ensure that high-scoring links stand out clearly while dense backgrounds fade back gracefully.
* `geom_node_point(aes(color = community_id, size = node_degree))` draws your proteins, scaling their physical size based on their hubness and coloring them dynamically across a high-contrast hue-chroma-luminance palette (`rainbow_hcl`).
* `guides(color = "none")` strips away the bulky text legends, keeping your plot area clean and production-ready.


5. **Data Backup Export (`saveRDS`)**: The script packages your live `tidygraph` network model, your optimized coordinates layout matrix, and your threshold criteria into a single serialized binary R object (`ssn_graph_object.rds`). This file lets you load your complete network state directly into RStudio at any time to adjust styling parameters dynamically without re-running the upstream pipeline.

### 1. `results/foldseek_alignments.tsv`

* **What it is:** The raw, unfiltered pairwise edge list output directly by the Foldseek structural alignment engine.
* **Internal Data Layout:** It is a tab-separated text spreadsheet containing seven core columns: `query` (Protein A ID), `target` (Protein B ID), `lddt` (0 to 1 score of local structural match), `prob` (alignment probability), `evalue` (statistical expectation value), `fident` (fractional sequence identity, 0 to 1), and `alntmscore` (TM-score of the structural alignment).
* **Notebook Interpretation:** This is your primary database. If your pipeline is configured to evaluate 870 proteins, this file tracks all pairs that passed the initial prefilter. It contains the raw structural and sequence metrics used to generate all downstream graphs.

### 2. `results/structural_similarity_matrix.tsv`

* **What it is:** A symmetric, square distance matrix populated entirely by local 3D geometric similarity scores (`lDDT`).
* **Internal Data Layout:** Both the row headers and column headers contain an identically sorted list of your 870 protein identifiers. The diagonal values are exactly `1.000` (since every protein matches itself perfectly). The intersections represent the precise mutual lDDT score between Protein X and Protein Y. If no alignment was detected between two highly diverged structures, the value drops to `0.000`.
* **Notebook Interpretation:** This file strips away the text wrappers and formats your entire dataset into a pristine mathematical tensor. This is the exact data layout required for modern unsupervised data science applications. You can load this file directly into Python or R to run Principal Component Analysis (PCA), t-SNE, UMAP, or to train a convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier to predict structural classifications.

### 3. `results/optimized_subfamily_assignments.tsv`

* **What it is:** The definitive biological master mapping ledger linking individual sequences to their mathematically optimized structural clades.
* **Internal Data Layout:** A clean tabular text sheet featuring three primary columns:
1. `protein_id`: The exact filename or accession handle of the source structural coordinate file.
2. `community_id`: An integer (e.g., 1 through 49) designating which Louvain community partition the protein belongs to at the peak modularity threshold.
3. `node_degree`: A raw integer counting the total number of high-confidence structural connections ($\text{lDDT} \ge 0.84$) that specific protein retains within the network.


* **Notebook Interpretation:** This is your operational guide for downstream laboratory validation.
* **To profile your structural subgroups:** Group the rows by `community_id` to instantly identify your largest newly resolved subfamilies. These represent the major stable structural variations within your dataset that were previously obscured inside the uncorrected network hairball.
* **To isolate structural anchors:** Filter for a specific subfamily clade and sort by `node_degree` in descending order. The proteins at the very top of this list possess the highest local centrality (they are the most connected "hubs" of that clade). These are your ideal candidates for high-resolution 3D tertiary structural overlays (using PyMOL or ChimeraX) or target selection for wet-lab biochemical characterization, as they represent the median structural template of that entire subfamily.



### 4. `results/optimized_subfamilies_network_map.pdf`

* **What it is:** A publication-ready, vector-graphic visualization of your complete Structural Similarity Network (SSN).
* **Internal Data Layout:** This high-resolution PDF contains the complete network layout arranged via deterministic stress-majorization. Individual points (nodes) are scaled in physical size based on their internal `node_degree` and colored across a balanced, high-contrast Hue-Chroma-Luminance (HCL) palette according to their `community_id`. The edges connecting the points are rendered with variable transparency proportional to their raw lDDT score, causing weak background connections to fade away while highly conserved structural lineages pop visually.
* **Notebook Interpretation:** This is the primary figure for your manuscript or thesis. Because it is exported as a true vector PDF, all node points, edge lines, and text elements are stored as pure geometric paths rather than flattened pixels. You can drop this file directly into vector editing suites like Adobe Illustrator, Inkscape, or CorelDRAW to alter line weights, adjust canvas dimensions, or add custom typographic annotations without losing image quality.

### 5. `results/sequence_identity_distribution.png`

* **What it is:** A high-resolution chart displaying the multi-modal distribution of fractional sequence identity (`fident`) across all structurally aligned pairs in your dataset.
* **Internal Data Layout:** A standard frequency histogram plotting Fractional Sequence Identity along the x-axis (ranging from `0.0` to `1.0`) against edge frequency along the y-axis.
* **Notebook Interpretation:** This graph provides critical evolutionary context for your network topology. When you see distinct, separated peaks (e.g., a dominant wave in the $12\% - 18\%$ "Twilight Zone" range and a secondary wave near $75\%$), it serves as empirical proof that your dataset represents an ancient, highly evolved enzyme superfamily. It demonstrates that while primary sequences have completely drifted across deep evolutionary time, nature has maintained tight structural conservation to preserve essential catalytic activity.

### 6. `results/ssn_graph_object.rds`

* **What it is:** A serialized R binary data object that packages the complete internal state of your network analysis.
* **Internal Data Layout:** This file compresses and saves an native R list structure containing three objects:
1. `graph`: The active `tbl_graph` object containing all raw node metadata and edge attributes.
2. `coordinates`: The exact coordinate layout matrix generated by the stress-majorization engine.
3. `cutoff`: The mathematically optimized lDDT value determined by your modularity sweep.


* **Notebook Interpretation:** This is your interactive sandbox file. Instead of running your entire high-throughput computational pipeline over again just to adjust a minor cosmetic detail on your graph (like testing a new color palette or highlighting a specific clade), you can load this single file instantly into an interactive RStudio session:
```R
ssn_data <- readRDS("results/ssn_graph_object.rds")

```
